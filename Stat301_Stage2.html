Pre-selection of Variables

The dataset contains variables that are likely highly correlated, specifically Height, Weight, and BMI. Using highly correlated predictors (multicollinearity) in a regression model can inflate standard errors and make inference unreliable.

Evidence for Multicollinearity: Before dropping variables, we examine the correlation matrix below to confirm redundancy.

  • There is a strong positive correlation (r 0.79) between Weight and Body Mass Index (BMI).
  • There is a moderate correlation (r 0.41) between Height and Weight.

Due to the strong correlation (0.79) between Weight and BMI, including both in the model would likely introduce multicollinearity, which would inflate the variance of the coefficient estimates and making them unstable.

Therefore, we will:

  • We will drop `Height` and `Weight`, as this information is already captured by `Body Mass Index (BMI)`.
  • We will drop `Total Body Water (TBW)` because it is a direct sum of `Extracellular Water (ECW)` and `Intracellular Water (ICW)`.
  • We will also drop any body composition measures like `Total Body Fat Ratio (TBFR) (%)`, `Lean Mass (LM) (%)`, `Obesity (%)`, and `Total Fat Content (TFC)` since we already have `BMI` and `Visceral Fat Area (VFA)` which will be enough for our purposes.

Focus: The primary focus is inference, as we want to understand which specific factors are associated with gallstone risk.

Section 4: Method and Plan

Proposed Method: Logistic Regression

To address our scientific question regarding the factors associated with gallstone presence, we will use a Logistic Regression model. We will model the probability of Gallstone Status which is the response based on patient demographics and biomarkers.

  • Appropriateness: Logistic regression is the standard method for binary classification problems where the response variable is binary (0 = Absent, 1 = Present). It allows us to estimate the odds ratios for each predictor.
  • Assumptions: We assume independence of observations and a linear relationship between the log-odds of the outcome and the continuous independent variables.
  • Limitations: Logistic regression can be sensitive to outliers and influential data points, it assumes that the omitted variables are not confounding the results.
